Yesterday I attended Google Cloud Korea’s Cloud Onboard event.

By now I feel relatively familiar with AWS, but I know much less about other cloud providers, and I did not want to become the kind of MLOps engineer who only knows one ecosystem. That was the reason I decided to sign up, and I still remember how happy I was when the confirmation email arrived soon afterward.

Of course, services such as GKE, Google Cloud’s managed Kubernetes offering, and AI playground-style services like Gemini and Vertex AI were interesting. But the part that stood out most to me was the early discussion on networking. The services closer to the user layer can sometimes be replaced by SaaS products or built directly, so if I were ever in the position to choose a platform for a project, I thought networking capabilities would be one of the main factors I would use to decide.

One of the networking services that was introduced in particular was Maglev. Maglev was presented under the title “A Fast and Reliable Software Network Load Balancer.” What made it memorable was the way it provides networking services with high performance and low latency. It was amusing to see that if you ask Gemini about it, it argues quite forcefully that it corresponds to AWS CloudFront.

I also appreciated that the event was designed to make first-time GCP learners feel comfortable, with Q&A sessions, refreshments, and an overall approachable flow. I am planning to attend another event hosted by GDG Songdo next week, and I think what I learned here will help me participate with a deeper understanding.
